Amazonians Unite!

Can Amazon's opponents plot an uprising in the middle of a pandemic?

Many of Amazon‚Äôs workers opted to stay home, leaving some warehouses nearly empty. Without its full workforce, Amazon‚Äôs ‚Äúeverything tomorrow‚Äù promise to customers evaporated. In the coronavirus age, Amazon is both mightier and more vulnerable than ever. For many of Amazon‚Äôs opponents, now looks like the time to strike.

On a late January afternoon, back when people still shook hands with strangers, a group of environmental activists, warehouse workers, and labor organizers handed out protest signs in a bowling alley parking lot in Southern California‚Äôs Inland Empire region (about an hour or two east of LA, depending on traffic). I had driven down to the warehouse-filled region to see the cutting edge of the fight against Amazon in person. Amazon has not yet been officially announced as the tenant for this facility, but the developer Hillwood Development Agencies is building a similar building for Amazon in Fort Worth, Texas, and every local media report about the San Bernardino facility mentions that Amazon is rumored to operate it once it opens. Despite employing tens of thousands of people in the Inland Empire, Amazon has no corporate offices in the area, so the protest was instead going to be at Hillwood‚Äôs offices, a short walk from the bowling alley parking lot.

At the beginning of 2020, pre-COVID, this is what the most visible fight against Amazon in the company‚Äôs largest US logistics hub looked like: a protest over a facility that hadn‚Äôt been built yet, at the office for the developer building it, who has still not publicly admitted to building that facility for Amazon. But I hadn‚Äôt only come to see Inland Empire residents protesting a new development. I hoped I could talk my way into Athena‚Äôs meetings before the protest, and witness them talking shop firsthand, but everyone I asked told me that the meetings were open to Athena member organizations only. One of Athena‚Äôs founders and the local protest‚Äôs organizers, Sheheryar Kaoosji, agreed to meet me a couple days before the protests at the crowded community organizing center where he worked (oh co-working spaces, the before-times...) for an interview, and to explain some of the wariness I was encountering.

On March 3rd, Amazon confirmed that its first employee to test positive for COVID-19 in the United States was not one of its warehouse workers, but a corporate employee at one of their headquarter buildings in Seattle, WA. Within two days, the company encouraged all of its Seattle-area corporate employees to work from home. But when states began issuing stay-at-home orders, and local businesses everywhere started shutting their doors, Amazon committed to keeping their delivery operation up and running, framing their work as essential. ‚ÄúGetting a priority item to your doorstep is vital as communities practice social-distancing, particularly for the elderly and others with underlying health issues,‚Äù wrote Dave Clark, Amazon‚Äôs Senior Vice President of Worldwide Operations, in a post on the company blog. The company quickly announced they would hire 100,000 new workers (since increased to 175,000) to meet the demand of those ordering goods from their home. They gave their employees a raise of $2 an hour for working in dangerous times and allowed workers to file for unlimited unpaid time off.

Some workers (Nate included) quickly decided the extra $2 would not justify the risk of catching a potentially fatal disease and decided to stay at home. But many still needed to pay their bills, so they kept going to work, nervous about co-workers getting closer than six feet, wishing that the company could get them masks and gloves sooner. Then some of Amazon‚Äôs warehouse workers started testing positive for COVID-19.

By the end of March, more than a dozen workers had tested positive at JFK8, Amazon‚Äôs warehouse in Staten Island, NY. (Amazon names each of its facilities with minimal creativity, slapping a number to the end of the three-letter code from a nearby airport.) On March 30th, workers staged a widely publicized walkout, demanding Amazon close JFK8, pay them better, and do more to keep them safe from coronavirus. The walkout made even more headlines after Amazon fired the walkout‚Äôs lead organizer, a 31-year-old assistant manager who had worked for Amazon for five years named Chris Smalls. Amazon said they fired Smalls for coming to the warehouse after he had been exposed to an infected coworker, but other workers with more direct exposure to coronavirus weren‚Äôt quarantined or fired. Smalls was certain the company fired him in retaliation for organizing the protest. At the time, I was making a radio story about how workers were responding to coronavirus cases at one warehouse in Southern California, and once word spread about Smalls getting fired any workers who were previously comfortable sharing their name asked to be anonymous.

I wanted to see how coronavirus had changed the priorities of organizers, so in early April I called up Kaoosji (who was no longer working from his co-working space) to hear what his team was doing to respond to the virus. Since social distancing recommendations preclude the tactics of traditional grassroots organizing, tactics like knocking on strangers‚Äô doors, gathering workers into rooms, and staging mass protests, Athena member organizations like Warehouse Worker Resource Center have learned to organize from home and think creatively about how they use the internet.
